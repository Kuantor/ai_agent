# British English

## British vs American Spelling

The main patterns, with the British form first:

- **-our / -or**: colour/color, favourite/favorite, honour/honor,
  neighbour/neighbor.
- **-re / -er**: centre/center, theatre/theater, metre/meter, litre/liter.
- **-ise / -ize**: realise/realize, organise/organize, apologise/apologize.
  (British publishers accept both; -ise is the safer everyday choice in the UK.)
- **-ll- / -l-**: travelling/traveling, cancelled/canceled, jewellery/jewelry.
- **-ence / -ense**: defence/defense, licence/license (noun).

Either system is correct English — but be consistent within one text. Exams
such as IELTS accept both; mixing them in one essay looks careless.

## British Grammar Habits

- **have got**: British speakers prefer "I've got a car" where Americans say
  "I have a car". Both are correct.
- **Collective nouns** can take plural verbs in British English: "The team
  are playing well" (BrE) vs "The team is playing well" (AmE).
- **Present perfect** is used more strictly in British English for recent
  events: "I've just eaten" (BrE) where an American might say "I just ate".
- **at the weekend** (BrE) vs "on the weekend" (AmE).

## Politeness and Understatement

British English is famously indirect. The polite machinery matters more than
in many languages — dropping it can sound rude even when your grammar is
perfect:

- Requests: "Could you possibly...?", "Would you mind...?", "I wonder if you
  could..." — rather than a bare imperative.
- Softening: "I'm afraid..." introduces bad news ("I'm afraid that's not
  quite right"). "Quite", "rather", "a bit" soften almost anything.
- Understatement: "not bad" often means "very good"; "a bit disappointing"
  can mean "terrible". Take British praise and criticism with calibration.
- Thanking and apologising happen constantly: the British apologise even
  when someone else steps on THEIR foot.

## Posh and Refined Turns of Phrase

Elevated British English you will meet in period dramas, formal writing, and
the speech of the well-mannered:

- "Shall we begin?" — an elegant invitation rather than "Let's start".
- "If I may..." — a courteous way to interject or offer advice.
- "splendid", "marvellous", "delightful" — refined praise.
- "rather" as an intensifier: "rather good", "rather a lot".
- "indeed" — agreement with gravitas.
- "Do forgive me" — a dressier "sorry".

Learners need not speak this way, but recognising the register helps with
literature, films, and understanding when an English speaker is being formal
— or gently ironic.
