# French Connections in English

## Why English Is Full of French Words

After the Norman Conquest of 1066, French became the language of the English
court, law, and cuisine for some three centuries. Roughly a third of English
vocabulary arrived from French or through French from Latin. This is why
English so often has two words for one idea: a plain everyday word from Old
English and a more formal one from French. Knowing this doubles your
vocabulary for free: if a word looks French, its more formal English meaning
is often guessable.

## Everyday vs Formal Word Pairs

The animal in the field kept its English name; the dish on the noble's table
took the French one:

| Everyday (Old English) | Formal (French origin) |
|------------------------|------------------------|
| cow / ox | beef (boeuf) |
| pig | pork (porc) |
| sheep | mutton (mouton) |
| ask | enquire / inquire (enquérir) |
| begin | commence (commencer) |
| buy | purchase (pourchasser) |
| help | aid / assist (aider) |
| freedom | liberty (liberté) |

In writing and polite speech, the French-derived word usually sounds more
formal. "May I enquire...?" is dressier than "Can I ask...?" — both are
correct English.

## Faux Amis: False Friends Between English and French

Words that look alike but mean different things. These also trip up
Ukrainian and Russian speakers because many entered Slavic languages through
French:

- **library** = бібліотека; but French **librairie** = bookshop.
- **actually** = насправді (in fact); but French **actuellement** = currently.
  The same trap as Russian "актуально" — English "actual" does NOT mean
  "topical/current".
- **magazine** = журнал (periodical); but French **magasin** = shop. Russian
  "магазин" (shop) follows the French meaning — English does not.
- **attend** = бути присутнім (be present at); but French **attendre** =
  to wait. "I attended the lecture" means you were there, not that you
  waited for it.
- **demand** in English is strong and insistent (вимагати); French
  **demander** is just to ask. Telling an English speaker "I demand..."
  sounds far ruder than intended.

## Suffix Patterns Borrowed from French

Recognising French suffixes helps you read unfamiliar words:

- **-tion / -sion**: nation, decision, translation — almost always the same
  in French, and usually близькі to the Ukrainian/Russian -ція/-ция words.
- **-age**: village, courage, language, luggage.
- **-able / -ible**: comfortable, possible — "able to be X-ed".
- **-ee vs -er**: employer (who hires) vs employee (who is hired); the -ee
  ending comes from the French past participle -é.

## Spelling Marks of French Origin

Silent letters and un-English spellings usually signal a French guest:
"receipt", "debt" (the b was added to match Latin), "colonel" (pronounced
"kernel"), "queue" (just the letter q, four-fifths silent), "bureau",
"rendezvous", "déjà vu". These words rarely follow English phonics rules —
learn them by sight, as the English themselves do.
